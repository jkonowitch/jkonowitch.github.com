People, not categories

July 9, 2013

I have been reading Sandi Metz’s Practical Object-Oriented Design in Ruby - a generally fantastic book - and I came across a passage that really struck me:

Domain objects are easy to find but they are not at the design center of your application. Instead, they are a trap for the unwary. If you fixate on domain objects you will tend to coerce behavior into them.

Sandi is referring to the design of your software at the code level, but it occurred to me that this insight applies to the larger level of the software itself and why it even exists. I often start the work of understanding a domain by enumerating the nouns that inhabit it, “Ok, so I know there is a customer and a manager and an office…” This is probably a decent strategy to get the ‘lay of the land’, but I think there is a potential pitfall. By categorizing and labeling ‘users’ too early, we can blind ourselves to the fact that these are human beings using our software.

We can ‘coerce behavior’ into our features when we imagine our users as categories and not people. It’s easy to import our stereotypes of what a “customer” is or a “manager” - the most generic words ever - and to design the software with these stodgy images in our minds. Far superior, I think, would be an approach that helps us humanize our users and focus on their specific qualities. What are these ‘customers’ like? What do they need, what do they struggle with, what do they want…who are they?!

PS - I remember hearing that some BDD practitioners develop extensive role narratives that use people’s names to substitute for generic categories like ‘customer’. Anyone know of any references or articles that discuss this? Please drop me a line.
